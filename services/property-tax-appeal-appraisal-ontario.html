What Can an Appraisal Do?

What Can a Property Tax Appeal Appraisal Do — and What Can't It Do?

A property tax appeal appraisal is an independent, certified opinion of a property's market value as of a specific assessment date. In Ontario, MPAC assesses properties based on market value at a legislated valuation date. An appraisal may provide evidence that a property's market value on that date was different from what MPAC has assessed.

What an appraisal can do: provide documented, CUSPAP-compliant market value evidence; support comparable sales analysis; and give an owner, their lawyer, or their tax agent a professionally credible basis for discussion or proceedings. An appraiser can also prepare a rebuttal to MPAC's analysis if one is provided.

What an appraisal cannot do: guarantee any change in assessment; predict the outcome of a Request for Reconsideration or ARB hearing; or provide legal or tax advice. Whether to proceed with an appeal, how to structure that appeal, and what procedural steps to take are decisions for qualified legal and tax professionals.

The Ontario Assessment System

MPAC, the Request for Reconsideration, and the Assessment Review Board

Ontario's property tax system uses assessed values set by MPAC. When a property owner believes their assessed value is inaccurate, two primary challenge mechanisms exist before formal appeal rights expire.

MPAC and the Request for Reconsideration (RfR)

MPAC (Municipal Property Assessment Corporation) is responsible for assessing all properties in Ontario. Property owners who disagree with their assessed value may file a Request for Reconsideration directly with MPAC. MPAC then reviews the assessment and responds with its findings.

An independent appraisal may be submitted to MPAC as part of this process. The appraisal provides market value evidence at the applicable valuation date, supported by documented comparable sales analysis and methodology.

Important note on assessment valuations: As of the current assessment cycle in Ontario, MPAC assessments are still based on a January 1, 2016 valuation date. Ontario's scheduled property reassessment has been postponed. Property owners should confirm the applicable valuation date with MPAC or a qualified professional before commissioning an appraisal for RfR purposes.

The Assessment Review Board (ARB)

The Assessment Review Board is an independent tribunal that hears appeals from property owners who remain unsatisfied after the RfR process, or who choose to appeal directly. ARB proceedings are more formal and typically involve exchange of written evidence and hearing dates.

In ARB proceedings, appraisal reports become part of the evidentiary record. Our appraisers have experience preparing reports that meet the disclosure and methodology requirements expected in tribunal settings. We can also provide expert testimony if required.

ARB appeals may be filed for commercial, industrial, multi-residential, and other property classes. The rules, timelines, and evidence requirements differ by property class and proceeding type. Legal advice is strongly recommended before filing.

How an Appraisal Helps

How Independent Market Value Evidence May Support an Assessment Dispute

Market Value Evidence

A CUSPAP-compliant appraisal documents market value at the applicable valuation date using verified comparable sales, income analysis, or cost evidence. This provides a professional, defensible basis for comparison against the MPAC assessed value.

Comparable Sales Analysis

We identify comparable properties that sold near the valuation date and analyze how they relate to the subject property. Where MPAC's comparables are inappropriate, a well-documented appraisal can present an alternative set with full analysis and adjustments.

Income and Expense Support

For income-producing properties, the income approach to value reflects actual rent levels, vacancy, operating expenses, and market capitalization rates. Where a property's income profile differs materially from MPAC's assumptions, documented income analysis may be relevant evidence.

A Critical Note on Deadlines

Deadlines to file a Request for Reconsideration or an ARB appeal vary by property class, notice type, and municipality. Missing a deadline may forfeit your right to challenge an assessment for the current cycle.

Do not assume your appeal window is open. Contact Metrix to discuss your situation before assuming timelines, and consult a qualified professional to confirm your procedural options and deadlines.

Getting Started

What Documents Are Helpful?

The more context you can provide, the more accurately we can scope the assignment. You do not need everything on this list to contact us.

  • Current MPAC assessment notice (Property Assessment Notice)
  • Municipal address and roll number
  • Rent roll and leases (income-producing properties)
  • Operating statements (2-3 years where available)
  • Any MPAC correspondence or data summary already received
  • Building size, age, condition, and any recent improvements
